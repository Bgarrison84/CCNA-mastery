CCNA Study Best Practices

Ten principles that separate candidates who pass from those who re-sit.

01
Follow the 6-week curriculum in order

Each week builds on the last. Skipping ahead creates gaps that compound — especially once routing and switching overlap in weeks 3–4.

02
Set a target exam date and work backwards

The app's Study Planner calculates your daily hours goal automatically. Cisco recommends ~300 hours total. Having a deadline makes it real.

03
Do CLI labs every week — muscle memory matters

The real exam includes simulation tasks. You need to type ip address 192.168.1.1 255.255.255.0 without thinking. The 36 CLI labs build that automaticity.

04
Use SRS daily — it's the most evidence-based retention method

Spaced repetition schedules reviews at the optimal forgetting threshold. Five minutes of SRS review each morning is worth more than an hour of re-reading notes.

05
Practice subnetting until /24–/30 splits take under 30 seconds

Subnetting questions appear throughout the exam and in simulation tasks. Slow subnetting burns time. Use the drill mode until it feels mechanical.

06
Take a full timed exam sim at the end of each week

Weekly exam sims surface weak domains before they become surprises. The per-domain breakdown tells you exactly where to focus next week.

07
Review every wrong answer before your next session

The Mistake Notebook auto-tracks every missed question. Reading the explanation immediately after a wrong answer is the single highest-ROI study action.

08
Supplement with Jeremy's IT Lab or the Official Cert Guide

This app tests and drills; it doesn't replace conceptual teaching. Jeremy's IT Lab on YouTube is free and outstanding. Cisco's OCG is the definitive written reference.

09
Join r/ccna — community context helps when concepts don't click

Thousands of people studying the same material. The sub has megathreads with explanations, mnemonics, and exam tips that no single app can replicate.

10
Aim for 85%+ on practice exams before booking the real thing

Cisco's pass score is 820/1000 (~82%). Building a buffer to 85%+ accounts for exam-day nerves and question variations you haven't seen. The Readiness Score in Stats tracks this gauge.
